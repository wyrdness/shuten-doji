# Shuten-dōji

> Shuten-dōji ('sake-drinking lad') is a legendary oni (demon) of Japanese folklore who, set in the reign of Emperor Ichijō (986–1011), is said to have led a band of demons from a fortress on Mount Ōe and abducted nobles of the capital. He was defeated by the warrior Minamoto no Yorimitsu (Raikō) and his Four Heavenly Kings, who infiltrated his stronghold disguised as yamabushi and gave him a poisoned sake.

## Overview

Shuten-dōji (酒呑童子, 'sake-drinking lad') is one of the most famous oni of Japanese legend. The story is set in the reign of Emperor Ichijō (986–1011) and casts the demon as the king of the oni of Mount Ōe (variously identified as Ōeyama in northwestern Kyoto / on the Tango–Tamba border, or with the southern Ōe near Kameoka). From this stronghold the demon and his followers are said to have raided the capital and carried off members of the nobility. By imperial command, the warrior Minamoto no Yorimitsu — popularly known as Raikō — together with his Four Heavenly Kings (shitennō: Watanabe no Tsuna, Sakata no Kintoki, Urabe no Suetake, and Usui Sadamitsu) and the courtier Fujiwara no Yasumasa, set out to destroy him. Disguising themselves as yamabushi (mountain ascetics) and aided by deities of Sumiyoshi, Iwashimizu Hachimangū, and Kumano (depending on the version), the heroes were welcomed into the demon's hall, where they presented him with a 'divine elixir' — a poisoned sake — that incapacitated him. Once he had drunk himself into a stupor they cut off his head, which is said to have flown through the air and tried to bite Raikō before being held down. The earliest surviving illustrated text, the Ōeyama Ekotoba (Ōeyama Picture Scroll), is held by the Itsuō Art Museum and dates to the 14th century. The legend was carried into Otogi-zōshi short stories, the Otogi Bunko of the early 18th century, Noh and Kabuki theatre, ukiyo-e, and into modern manga, anime, and video games. Major variant texts include Ōeyama Shuten-dōji and Ibukiyama Shuten-dōji, which place the events at different mountains and offer different origin stories for the demon.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Japanese: 酒 shu ('sake'), 呑 ten ('to drink'), 童子 dōji ('boy / child / acolyte').
- **Meaning**: 'Sake-drinking lad', referring to the demon's love of sake.
- **First use**: 14th century (in the Ōeyama Ekotoba); the legend is set earlier, in the Heian period (Emperor Ichijō, r. 986–1011).

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Real demon king of Heian Japan** _(Tradition; Heian-court legend literature)_

Within tradition, Shuten-dōji is treated as a real demon-king who menaced the Heian capital and was destroyed by Yorimitsu's expedition.

### Skeptical

**Allegory for marginalised peoples and bandits** _(Noriko T. Reider, Komatsu Kazuhiko)_

Modern scholarship has read Shuten-dōji as a folkloric figure encoding Heian and medieval anxieties about non-court populations of the mountains — bandits, mountain ascetics, ironworking communities, and groups outside the capital's authority — recast as 'demons'.

**Heroic legitimation of the Minamoto** _(Medieval Japanese literary historians)_

The Yorimitsu cycle, including Shuten-dōji, functioned to legitimise the warrior aristocracy of the Minamoto and their service to the imperial court.
