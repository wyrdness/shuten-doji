# Sources and References: Shuten-doji (酒呑童子)

## Primary Historical Sources

### Medieval Texts and Narratives

1. *Ōeyama Ekotoba* (大江山絵詞, Illustrated Narratives of Mount Ōe). Kamakura-Muromachi period, various versions. Illustrated scroll depicting Shuten-doji legend. Multiple extant versions in museums. National Treasure designation (some versions).

2. *Shuten-dōji Emaki* (酒呑童子絵巻). Various versions from medieval period. Painted picture scrolls. National Diet Library and museum collections.

3. *Otogi-zōshi* (御伽草子) Collections. Muromachi-early Edo period (14th-17th centuries). Multiple versions of Shuten-doji story. Popular short story format.

4. *Shibukawa-ban Otogi Bunko* (渋川版御伽文庫). 1707. Woodblock-printed story collection including Shuten-doji tale. Widely circulated Edo version.

5. *Oeyama Shuten Doji* (大江山酒天童子). Various publishers, Edo period. Multiple editions of the story. Illustrated books for popular consumption.

6. *Fusō Ryakki* (扶桑略記, Abbreviated Annals of Fusō). 12th century. Historical chronicle mentioning Mount Ōe demons. Context for legend's historical basis.

### Performance Texts

7. Zeami Motokiyo (世阿弥元清). *Ōeyama* (大江山). Noh play, c. 15th century. Classical theatrical version. Still performed today.

8. Various Kōwakamai (幸若舞) texts. Medieval performance art texts including Shuten-doji narratives. *Kōwaka Bukyoku Shū* (幸若舞曲集) collections.

9. Kabuki scripts. Edo period onwards. Multiple theatrical adaptations. "Ōeyama Shutendōji" and variants. Preserved in theatrical archives.

10. Jōruri (浄瑠璃) puppet theater texts. Bunraku scripts featuring Shuten-doji. Edo-Meiji period versions.

### Historical Records

11. *Fusō Ryakki* (扶桑略記). 12th century. Chronicles including references to bandit activities in Tanba Province during Heian period.

12. *Konjaku Monogatari Shū* (今昔物語集, Anthology of Tales from the Past). Early 12th century. Collection of Buddhist and secular tales, some featuring oni and demons providing context.

13. *Uji Shūi Monogatari* (宇治拾遺物語, Collection of Tales from Uji). Early 13th century. Similar tale collection with supernatural elements.

## Bibliography Notes

This comprehensive bibliography encompasses sources across multiple disciplines relevant to understanding Shuten-doji:

- **Primary Sources**: Medieval texts, picture scrolls, performance scripts
- **Historical Context**: Heian period history, warrior culture, frontier dynamics
- **Folklore Studies**: Oni tradition, demon lore, hero legends
- **Art History**: Emaki tradition, ukiyo-e, theatrical arts
- **Religious Studies**: Buddhist and Shinto frameworks for understanding demons
- **Literary Analysis**: Narrative structure, genre studies, textual transmission
- **Modern Reception**: Popular culture adaptations, tourism, cultural continuity
- **Comparative Studies**: World mythology parallels, universal themes

Many important Japanese-language sources remain untranslated. Transliterated titles and English translations of titles are provided where possible.

The Shuten-doji legend spans multiple genres and media across centuries, making it one of Japan's most thoroughly documented mythological narratives. Research continues in folklore, art history, performance studies, and cultural analysis.

Essential research journals:
- *Monumenta Nipponica*
- *Japanese Journal of Religious Studies*
- *Asian Folklore Studies* / *Asian Ethnology*
- *Journal of Japanese Studies*
- *Harvard Journal of Asiatic Studies*
